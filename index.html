FAQ

Freight calculation questions

How do you calculate CBM?

CBM = length × width × height, with every dimension in metres. A 120 cm × 100 cm × 80 cm carton is 1.2 × 1.0 × 0.8 = 0.96 CBM. Multiply by the number of cartons for the total shipment volume. Always use the outer packed dimensions of the carton, measured at the widest point.

What is the volumetric weight divisor for air freight?

Air freight uses the IATA volumetric factor of 6000 cm³/kg, which is the same as 167 kg per cubic metre. So an air shipment of 3 CBM has a volumetric weight of 501 kg. Courier and express carriers commonly use 5000 cm³/kg, which equals 200 kg per CBM.

How many CBM is a 20ft container?

A standard 20ft container has an internal volume of about 33 CBM, with a realistic usable loading capacity of 25 to 28 CBM. A 40ft standard is about 67 CBM internal and 55 to 58 CBM usable. A 40ft High Cube is about 76 CBM internal and 62 to 66 CBM usable. Usable capacity is lower because of pallet footprints, stacking limits and dunnage.

At what volume does FCL become cheaper than LCL?

The break-even is typically between 13 and 15 CBM on most trade lanes. Below that, LCL groupage billed per cubic metre is usually cheaper. Above it, a flat-rate 20ft FCL container tends to win, and it also avoids LCL consolidation delays and extra destination handling charges.

What is a loading metre (LDM)?

A loading metre is one metre of trailer floor space across the full 2.4 metre width of a standard European trailer. LDM = (length in metres × width in metres) ÷ 2.4 per item. European road freight is priced per loading metre, and one LDM is conventionally converted to 1750 kg of chargeable weight.
